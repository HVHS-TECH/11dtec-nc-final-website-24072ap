A black hole is a region in space where gravity becomes so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape once it gets too close. It usually forms when a very massive star collapses in on itself at the end of its life, squeezing all its mass into an extremely small, dense point called a singularity. Surrounding this is the event horizon, which is the boundary beyond which escape is impossible. Even though black holes can’t be seen directly, scientists know they exist because of how they affect nearby stars and gas, pulling them in and often heating that material so it emits radiation before crossing the event horizon.

Stars are giant balls of hot gas – mostly hydrogen, with some helium and small amounts of other elements. Every star has its own life cycle, ranging from a few million to trillions of years, and its properties change as it ages.z Stars form in large clouds of gas and dust called molecular clouds. Molecular clouds range from 1,000 to 10 million times the mass of the Sun and can span as much as hundreds of light-years. Molecular clouds are cold which causes gas to clump, creating high-density pockets. Some of these clumps can collide with each other or collect more matter, strengthening their gravitational force as their mass grows. Eventually, gravity causes some of these clumps to collapse. When this happens, friction causes the material to heat up, which eventually leads to the development of a protostar, a baby star. After a few billion years, a low-mass star’s atmosphere will keep expanding until it becomes a subgiant or giant star while fusion converts helium into carbon in the core. (This will be the fate of our Sun, in several billion years.) Some giants become unstable and pulsate, periodically inflating and ejecting some of their atmospheres. Eventually, all the star’s outer layers blow away, creating an expanding cloud of dust and gas called a planetary nebula.
